Peru votes in election shaped by ex-leader’s legacy

by Julianne McCormick on June 3rd

In a tight presidential runoff election Sunday, Peruvians seemed equally divided between two very different candidates: the daughter of imprisoned ex-president Alberto Fujimori, and her older opponent, former World Bank economist Pedro Pablo Kuczynski.

Two exit polls on Sunday suggested that the 77-year-old Kuczynski would narrowly defeat Keiko Fujimori, though a third poll gave Fujimori a narrow lead, the Associated Press reported.

The vote was seen largely as a referendum on the legacy of Alberto Fujimori, an iron-handed ruler who led the nation for a decade in the 1990s. He is serving a 25-year sentence for corruption, among other charges.

Keiko Fujimori, 41, originally seemed headed to easy victory: she won the first round of voting by a 20 percentage-point margin, but by Sunday, her wide lead over Kuczynski had disappeared.

In a quick count of 84.5% of sample ballots, pollster Ipsos-Apoyo said Kuczynski won 50.9% of votes, with Fujimori winning 49.1%, Reuters reported.

Another local pollster, Gfk, which was the most accurate in predicting results from the first round, showed Kuczynski winning by more than two percentage points, while a third poll by CPI showed Fujimori winning by three points.

Official results aren't expected until after 10 p.m. ET.

As exit polls demonstrated the tight race, a jubilant Fujimori waved to supporters from a hotel terrace in Lima. Meanwhile, Kuczynski's campaign aides filed into his home to await official results, flashing "V' for victory" signs.

A potential swing vote in a close race could be the 885,000 Peruvians eligible to vote abroad — about 3.8% of the electorate, AP reported.

Kuczynski, known widely as "PPK," supported Fujimori in a 2011 presidential runoff, won by outgoing President Ollanta Humala. But he has campaigned aggressively against his younger opponent, telling supporters at a rally last week in Lima, "Peru is on the threshold of becoming a narco-state."

The reference wasn’t just to the elder Fujimori’s well-known ties to corruption, organized crime and death squads, but an attempt to draw attention to a string of scandals that have hobbled the younger Fujimori in the final stretch — most notably a report that one of her big fundraisers and the secretary general of her party is the target of a U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration investigation, AP reported.

Peru is the world’s largest producer of cocaine.

Kuczynski benefited from a last-minute endorsement by the third-place finisher in the first round of voting, leftist congresswoman Veronika Mendoza, the protagonist of a massive anti-Fujimori demonstration this week.
